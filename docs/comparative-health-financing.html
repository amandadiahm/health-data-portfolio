Two Systems, Two Strategies: Health Financing and Universal Coverage in Australia and Thailand

Author

Amanda Diah Maharani

Published

April 6, 2026

Introduction

Health financing systems play a critical role in protecting individuals from financial risk, particularly where out-of-pocket payments remain a major source of funding. Sustainable health financing requires diversified and pooled funding that allocates costs according to ability to pay rather than need [1].

This essay compares the health financing systems of two countries, Australia and Thailand. The comparison focuses on differences in major health financing sources, including their historical and political contexts, key reforms over the past two decades and approaches to financial protection for the poor. The comparison results are summarised in Table 1 and the following sections of this essay will elaborate on these results further.

Primary Sources of Health Financing

Australia and Thailand’s health financing arrangements differ substantially in funding sources. According to the Health Expenditure Australia 2023-24 data, Australia’s health financing system is predominantly tax-funded, with a significant role for private health insurance and out-of-pocket (OOP) payments, reflecting its mixed public-private model (Table 1) [2] Meanwhile, Thailand’s health financing system relies on a more centralised public financing model, in which almost 70% of the Universal Coverage Scheme (UCS) is funded by general government revenues [3]. The system is organised around three public insurance schemes, with the tax-financed UCS serving as the dominant platform for coverage and financial protection [3, 4].

Historical and Political Context

The contrasting financing models of Australia and Thailand reflect their distinct historical and political trajectories. Australia’s approach to Universal Health Coverage (UHC) has developed gradually through political compromise. Medicare, introduced in the 1980s, is a tax-financed framework that guarantees access to public hospital services and subsidised medical care [5]. At the same time, governments chose not to dismiss the existing private insurance sector. Instead, Commonwealth subsidies and tax incentives were maintained to support private insurance, embedding a mixed public-private financing model [6]. Australia’s federal structure has further shaped this arrangement, with funding and service responsibilities split between the Commonwealth and the states, particularly for hospitals [5].

On the other hand, Thailand’s experience with UHC is characterised by rapid change driven by political momentum and explicit equity goals. Before the early 2000s, health coverage in Thailand was fragmented, with multiple insurance schemes that left large segments of the population, especially informal sector workers, without adequate protection [7]. The introduction of the UCS in 2002 was built on years of policy development by public health experts and was propelled by electoral competition that elevated universal health care to a national priority [8]. Policymakers adopted a tax financed model that redefined access to health care as an entitlement. This approach was closely tied to cost-containment measures, reflecting the fiscal constraints of a middle-income country [8].

Major Health Financing Reforms

Over the past two decades, Australia and Thailand have implemented significant health financing reforms. In Australia, reforms have focused on improving efficiency and accountability within an established universal system [2]. The National Health Reform Agreement (NHRA) and the introduction of the ABF in 2011 aimed to improve efficiency, interstate equity and transparency in health care expenditures [2, 9, 10]. Further changes under the Federation Funding Agreement after 2020 sought to streamline fragmented Commonwealth–state funding arrangements and clarify roles in hospital and preventive service financing [2]. Moreover, recent Strengthening Medicare reforms (2022–2025) have targeted primary care affordability, workforce shortages and system integrity [11, 12].

On the other hand, Thailand was rapidly expanding UHC in the early 2000s. The tax financed model prioritised cost containment through capitation, global budgets and strong primary care gatekeeping, allowing rapid coverage expansion while maintaining fiscal control [13]. Framed as an equity driven nation building project, the UCS explicitly sought to reduce disparities in access and financial risk that had left around 30% of the population uninsured prior to 2001 [3]. More recent reforms in 2020 under the SAFE (Sustainable, Affordable, Fair and Efficient) framework addressed rising costs, population aging and benefit disparities across insurance schemes [14].

The differing nature of these reforms can be explained by each country’s institutional and political context. Australia already has universal coverage established, alongside a strong private insurance sector and a federal system that promoted incremental reform [2]. Consequently, reforms focus on system refinement and workforce sustainability rather than redistribution [2]. Thailand, by contrast, pursued UHC as a nation building project at a moment of political opportunity, enabling more decisive reforms centered on equity and financial risk protection [8].

Financial Protection for Vulnerable Populations

As Australia and Thailand commit to UHC, the degree of financial protection afforded to poor households differs substantially between the two systems. In Australia, the health financing system suggests a strong foundation for protecting households from excessive health care costs. Medicare provides universal, tax financed access to public hospital care free at the point of use, while also subsidising medical services and medicines through the Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme (PBS) [12]. In addition, the Australian government’s subsidies for private health insurance aim to increase consumer choice and reduce demand for public hospitals. However, research suggests that these subsidies have only a limited effect on overall system capacity [15, 16]. Evidence points to persistent affordability challenges in primary care. Declining bulk-billing rates and fee-for-service pricing have increased OOP costs, yet gaps in routinely reported financial risk indicators limit the assessment of Medicare’s equity performance [17-20].

Thailand’s financial protection outcomes are more clearly documented. Only about 2% of Thai households spend more than 10% of their total consumption on health care, which is among the lowest rates of catastrophic health expenditure in the region [3, 21]. This reflects near-universal enrolment across public schemes (Table 1) [21]. Key structural features include minimal or zero user fees, capitation payments for outpatient care, DRG with global budgets for inpatient services and a strong primary care gatekeeping system that reduces reliance on costly hospital care [8].

As summarised in Table 1, Thailand offers stronger financial protection for the poor by combining explicit equity objectives, comprehensive tax financing and strict cost control mechanisms. Meanwhile, Australia lacks recently published financial risk indicators, which prevents direct comparison. These differences reflect broader structural and policy choices: Thailand’s reforms prioritised reducing financial hardship and expanding equity, while Australia has focused on system efficiency, digital modernisation and workforce capacity rather than directly lowering OOP costs.

Policy Recommendations and Challenges

Evidence indicates that financial protection for low income populations can be strengthened through targeted health financing reforms. Australia’s recent investments under the Strengthening Medicare agenda reflect government recognition of long term underinvestment in primary care and persistent workforce shortages. Rising OOP costs and workforce gaps stem from interconnected structural pressures, including growing demand for care, insufficient remuneration in general practice, workforce maldistribution, and the flexibility of fee for service pricing under Medicare [5, 22].

Expanding bulk billing incentives in primary care therefore, offers clear equity benefits. Reducing gap fees lessens the immediate financial burden on disadvantaged households, who are more likely to delay care due to cost [19]. Improved affordability of first contact care can also reduce avoidable hospital admissions by supporting earlier management of chronic conditions and, over time, lowering health inequalities and subsequent health system costs [20, 22, 23]. Nonetheless, implementation challenges remain. Workforce shortages—particularly in rural and remote areas—may limit practices’ capacity to meet increased demand, while inadequate indexation of incentives may continue to deter bulk billing [12, 16, 18].

In Thailand, strengthening financial protection requires harmonising benefit packages across the public insurance schemes. Despite strong overall financial protection, disparities in entitlements generate inequities in access to and quality of care, particularly for UCS beneficiaries, who are predominantly low-income or informal-sector workers [8, 21]. Harmonisation would enhance equity by aligning access to essential and high cost services with health needs rather than employment status, limiting incentives for cost shifting. Benefit and payment alignment could improve efficiency and reduce fragmentation [13, 24]. However, progress is constrained by political resistance from CSMBS beneficiaries, who receive more generous service entitlements, as well as the rising fiscal pressures linked to population aging [3, 8, 25].

Conclusion

Overall, the comparison between Australia and Thailand illustrates how differences in health financing design can lead to markedly different outcomes in equity and financial protection. The UHC alone does not guarantee equity or effective financial protection. Thailand’s tax financed UCS, supported by strong cost control mechanisms, has delivered clear protection against financial hardship for low income and informal sector populations [8]. Australia’s mixed financing model ensures broad access in principle but continues to face affordability, workforce, and coordination challenges. Aligning health financing reforms more explicitly with equity objectives remains essential to ensuring that universal coverage translates into meaningful, affordable access to care for those most at risk.
